Starting piano lessons can feel daunting — especially if you've never played an instrument before. Whether you're an adult beginner or a parent considering lessons for your child, here's an honest look at what those first weeks and months actually involve.

## Your Very First Lesson

The first lesson is about getting comfortable. There's no test, no pressure, and no expectation that you'll know anything at all. Here's what typically happens:

**We'll have a chat.** Your teacher will ask about your musical interests, what you'd like to achieve, and whether you have any experience. This helps shape the direction of your lessons from the start.

**You'll sit at the piano.** We'll show you the basics — how to sit, where to place your hands, and how to find middle C. You'll start making sounds on your very first visit.

**You'll play something simple.** By the end of your first lesson, you'll have played a short melody with one hand. It won't be Beethoven, but it will be music — and it will feel surprisingly satisfying.

## The First Month

The first four weeks are about building foundations. This is where you learn the skills that everything else will rest on:

- **Hand position and posture** — how to sit correctly, how to hold your hands, and how to use your fingers efficiently
- **Reading basic notation** — learning to recognise notes on the stave and match them to keys on the piano
- **Playing with each hand separately** — starting with simple melodies in the right hand and basic patterns in the left
- **Rhythm and counting** — understanding how to keep time and play notes for the right duration

It might feel slow, but resist the urge to rush. These fundamentals make the difference between steady progress and getting stuck later.

## Months Two and Three

This is where things start to get exciting. You'll begin:

- **Playing with both hands together** — the moment most beginners find both challenging and thrilling
- **Learning about chords** — understanding how notes combine to create harmony
- **Tackling real pieces** — short pieces that actually sound like music, not just exercises
- **Building basic scales** — the patterns that underpin almost everything you'll play

For most students, this is when the first real buzz kicks in. The feeling of playing a piece from start to finish — even a simple one — is genuinely rewarding.

## By Six Months

With regular weekly lessons and consistent practice (even just 15–20 minutes a day), most beginners will have:

- A small repertoire of 5–10 pieces they can play
- A working knowledge of music theory basics
- The ability to read simple music at sight
- Enough technique to start exploring different styles
- The option to attempt Grade 1 or 2 pieces if they wish

Progress varies from person to person, and there's no rush. Some students move faster, others prefer to take their time — both approaches are perfectly fine.

## Do You Need a Piano at Home?

Yes — you'll need something to practise on between lessons. Without regular practice, progress will be much slower regardless of how good your lessons are.

You don't need a grand piano. A **full-size digital piano with weighted keys** is an excellent starting point and costs around €400–€700. Weighted keys are important because they simulate the resistance of a real piano, which means the technique you develop at home transfers properly to your lessons.

We're always happy to recommend specific models if you need guidance.

## How Much Practice Do You Need?

Less than you might think:

- **Children (ages 5–7):** 10–15 minutes a day
- **Children (ages 8+):** 15–30 minutes a day
- **Adults:** 15–20 minutes a day is a great starting point

The key is **consistency, not duration**. Fifteen minutes every day is far more effective than an hour once a week. We give clear practice goals after every lesson so you always know what to work on.

## Common Worries (That You Don't Need to Have)

**"I'm not musical."** Almost everyone who says this turns out to be wrong. Music is a skill, not a talent — it can be taught, and you can learn it.

**"My fingers won't work."** They will. Piano requires coordination, not extraordinary flexibility. Your hands will adapt within weeks of regular playing.

**"I can't read music."** Nobody can when they start. You'll learn alongside your playing, and it's much easier than most people expect.

**"I'll be terrible."** You'll be a beginner, which is completely different from being terrible. Every pianist in the world started exactly where you are now.
