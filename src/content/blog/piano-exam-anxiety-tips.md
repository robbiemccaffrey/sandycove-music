If you get nervous before a piano exam, you're not alone. Performance anxiety affects pianists of every age and level — from a 7-year-old taking their first RIAM Preliminary exam to a diploma candidate who's been playing for fifteen years.

The good news is that exam nerves are manageable. You can't eliminate them entirely (and a little adrenaline actually helps you perform better), but you can stop them from derailing your performance. Here's how.

## Why Do We Get Nervous?

Understanding what's happening in your body helps you deal with it. When you're anxious, your brain perceives a threat — in this case, the possibility of making mistakes in front of an examiner — and triggers your fight-or-flight response.

This floods your system with adrenaline, which causes the physical symptoms you know well: shaky hands, a racing heart, shallow breathing, sweaty palms, a dry mouth, and that horrible feeling in your stomach.

The important thing to understand is that this response is automatic and normal. It doesn't mean you're not prepared. It doesn't mean you're going to play badly. It just means your body is responding to a high-stakes situation in the way it's designed to.

## Preparation: The Foundation of Confidence

The single most effective way to reduce exam anxiety is thorough preparation. When you know your pieces inside out, your muscle memory can carry you even when your conscious mind is racing.

### Know Your Pieces Beyond "Good Enough"

There's a difference between being able to play your pieces and being exam-ready. Exam-ready means:

- You can play each piece from memory (if required) without hesitation
- You can start from any section, not just the beginning
- You can recover from a mistake without stopping
- You've played the pieces at the correct tempo consistently for at least two weeks

If a piece still feels shaky the week before the exam, it's not ready. Talk to your teacher about whether to simplify your programme or request a later exam date.

### Do Mock Exams

Mock exams are one of the most effective tools for managing anxiety, and we use them regularly at Sandycove School of Music. A mock exam replicates the real experience: you walk into the room, sit down, and perform your full programme without stopping.

The first time you do a mock exam, it feels nerve-wracking. By the third or fourth time, it feels routine. That's exactly the point — you're training your brain to recognise the exam situation as familiar rather than threatening.

Ask your teacher to run mock exams, or set up your own at home. Play your programme for family members, friends, or even a phone camera. The more you practise performing, the less frightening the real thing becomes.

### Record Yourself

Recording yourself playing and listening back is uncomfortable at first, but it's incredibly useful. It helps you:

- Hear mistakes you miss while playing
- Get used to the pressure of "this take matters"
- Build confidence when you hear yourself playing well
- Identify specific sections that need more work

Make recording a regular part of your [practice routine](/blog/how-to-practice-piano-effectively). By exam day, you'll be completely comfortable with the idea of someone listening critically to your playing.

## The Week Before the Exam

The final week is not the time to make big changes. If you're still learning notes at this stage, something has gone wrong with your preparation timeline.

**Do:**
- Run through your full programme once a day, start to finish
- Practise your scales, arpeggios, and sight-reading at a comfortable pace
- Keep your practice sessions shorter than usual — 20-30 minutes of focused work is plenty
- Get enough sleep, eat well, and stay physically active

**Don't:**
- Cram or extend your practice hours dramatically
- Try to re-finger passages or change your interpretation
- Obsess over that one tricky bar — if it's not secure by now, accept it and move on
- Read horror stories about exams online

## On the Day: Practical Tips

### Before You Arrive

- Eat a proper meal at least an hour before the exam. Low blood sugar makes anxiety worse.
- Arrive early enough to feel settled, but not so early that you're sitting around getting more nervous.
- Warm up your hands gently. Cold, stiff fingers don't help anyone.

### Breathing Exercises

This is the single most effective on-the-day technique for managing nerves. Before you go into the exam room, try this:

1. Breathe in slowly through your nose for 4 counts
2. Hold for 4 counts
3. Breathe out slowly through your mouth for 6 counts
4. Repeat 4-5 times

This activates your parasympathetic nervous system — the opposite of fight-or-flight — and physically calms your body. It works remarkably quickly.

### In the Exam Room

- Take a moment before you start playing. Adjust the bench, place your hands on the keys, and breathe.
- Don't rush into your first piece. The examiner isn't timing you with a stopwatch.
- If you make a mistake, keep going. Examiners expect minor slips — they're looking at the overall performance, not counting errors.
- Focus on the music, not the examiner. Pretend you're playing for yourself at home.

### Dealing with a Mistake Mid-Performance

Every pianist makes mistakes in exams. What matters is how you handle them.

**If you hit a wrong note:** Keep going. Don't react, don't grimace, don't stop. Most wrong notes are less noticeable than you think, especially if the rhythm and musical flow continue.

**If you lose your place:** Find the nearest natural starting point (the beginning of the next phrase or section) and pick up from there. A brief pause is far better than going back to the beginning.

**If your mind goes blank:** Stop. Breathe. Place your hands in the position for the next section you can remember. Start from there. Examiners have seen this many times and they will wait patiently.

## How Teachers Can Help

If you're a parent reading this, or a student wondering what more your teacher could be doing, here are some things a good teacher should provide in the lead-up to exams:

- **Regular mock exams** in realistic conditions
- **A clear preparation timeline** so pieces are ready well before the exam date
- **Strategies for recovery** — practising what to do when things go wrong
- **Perspective and reassurance** — reminding students that one exam doesn't define them
- **Familiarity with the exam board** — knowing exactly what [RIAM, ABRSM, or Trinity](/blog/riam-vs-abrsm-vs-trinity) examiners are looking for

## A Word About Perspective

Exam nerves are worst when the stakes feel too high. If a student believes that their entire musical worth depends on this one exam, the pressure becomes unbearable.

It helps to remember: a grade exam is a snapshot of where you are right now. It's a useful benchmark, a motivating goal, and a chance to perform — but it's not a judgement on your talent or potential.

Students who enjoy exams (and many do, eventually) tend to be the ones who see them as an opportunity to show what they can do, rather than a test they might fail.
