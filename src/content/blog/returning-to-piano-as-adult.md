You played piano as a child. Maybe you got to Grade 3, or Grade 5, or even Grade 8. Then life happened — school exams, college, work, family — and the piano gathered dust. Now, years or even decades later, you're thinking about picking it up again.

You're not alone. A huge number of our adult students at Sandycove School of Music are returners rather than complete beginners. And the experience of coming back to piano is often very different from what people expect.

## The Common Fears

Let's address the worries that keep people from restarting, because almost all of them are overblown.

### "I've Forgotten Everything"

You haven't. You really haven't. When you sit at a piano after years away, you'll be surprised by how much comes back. Your fingers will find their way to familiar patterns. You'll recognise notes on the stave more quickly than you expect. Scales you practised hundreds of times as a child will start to resurface within a session or two.

This is because of motor memory — the same mechanism that means you never truly forget how to ride a bicycle. The neural pathways you built through years of practice are still there. They're rusty, but they're not gone.

### "I'm Too Old"

No, you're not. We've had students return to piano in their 40s, 50s, 60s, and beyond. Your fingers might be a little stiffer than they were at twelve, but they're more than capable of playing piano. You might need to warm up more carefully and build flexibility gradually, but age is not the barrier people think it is.

What you have now that you didn't have as a child is motivation, patience, life experience with music, and a real appreciation for what you're doing. These advantages more than compensate for any physical changes.

### "My Hands Won't Work Properly"

Unless you have a specific medical condition affecting your hands, they'll be fine. Piano doesn't require extreme strength or flexibility. The coordination and fine motor control you need will come back with practice — usually faster than you expect.

If you do experience discomfort, stiffness, or pain when you start playing again, that's a sign to slow down and focus on proper technique, not to give up. A good teacher will help you build up gradually and avoid strain. Our [guide to piano posture and hand position](/blog/piano-posture-hand-position-guide) covers this in detail.

### "I'll Be Embarrassed Starting Again"

There's nothing embarrassing about returning to something you love. A good teacher will meet you exactly where you are, not where you "should" be. There's no judgement — only encouragement.

Many of our returning adult students say their biggest regret is not coming back sooner.

## What Muscle Memory Actually Retains

Here's what typically happens when an adult returner sits at a piano for the first time in years:

**Immediately familiar:** The layout of the keyboard. The feel of the keys. Basic hand positions. The sound of major and minor chords.

**Comes back within days:** Simple scales and arpeggios. The ability to read treble clef notation (bass clef often takes a bit longer). Basic coordination between the hands.

**Comes back within weeks:** More complex scales and technical patterns. Pieces you played repeatedly as a child (even if imperfectly). A sense of musical phrasing and expression.

**Takes longer to rebuild:** Speed and agility. Sight-reading fluency. The stamina to play for extended periods. Advanced technical passages.

The general rule is that the more you practised something originally, the faster it comes back. If you drilled your scales every day for five years, they'll return quickly. If you barely practised between lessons, there's less muscle memory to draw on — but you'll still progress faster than a complete beginner.

## How Quickly Can You Get Back to Where You Were?

This varies enormously, but here are some rough benchmarks based on what we've seen:

**If you played to Grade 3-4 and stopped:** You might be playing at that level again within 2-3 months of regular practice and weekly lessons.

**If you played to Grade 5-6 and stopped:** Expect 3-6 months to get back to a comparable level, depending on how much you practise.

**If you played to Grade 7-8 and stopped:** The basics will come back quickly, but the advanced repertoire and technical demands will take 6-12 months to fully rebuild.

These are rough estimates. Some people progress faster, some slower. The key variable is consistent practice — even 20 minutes a day makes an enormous difference.

## Setting Realistic Goals

One of the most important things to do when returning to piano is to set goals that motivate you without setting you up for frustration.

**Good goals for the first few months:**
- Relearn a favourite piece from your childhood
- Work through the scales and arpeggios for a specific grade
- Play one new piece that you've always wanted to learn
- Build a consistent daily practice habit

**Goals that can come later:**
- Take a grade exam (many adults find this surprisingly motivating)
- Learn to play a specific genre you never explored (jazz, contemporary, film music)
- Join an ensemble or accompany a singer
- Perform at an informal recital

Don't try to pick up exactly where you left off. If you were working on Grade 6 pieces when you stopped, start with Grade 3 or 4 material and work your way back up. This builds confidence, reinforces fundamentals, and avoids the frustration of struggling with pieces that are temporarily beyond your current ability.

## Choosing the Right Pieces to Restart With

Your teacher will help you choose appropriate repertoire, but here are some principles:

**Start below your old level.** Playing pieces that feel comfortable and achievable is far more motivating than struggling with material that's too difficult. You'll progress through them quickly, which builds momentum.

**Choose pieces you enjoy.** As an adult, you get to choose what you play. If you hated the pieces you were given as a child, pick something you actually want to learn. That intrinsic motivation is your biggest advantage.

**Include some technique work.** Scales, arpeggios, and simple exercises might not be exciting, but they're the fastest way to rebuild finger strength, coordination, and familiarity with the keyboard. Think of them as warm-ups rather than chores.

**Mix familiar and new.** Revisiting a piece you played years ago is a wonderful experience — your fingers remember more than you think. But also learn something completely new, so you're not just reliving the past.

## What's Different About Learning as an Adult?

If you last had lessons as a child, the experience of learning as an adult is quite different — and mostly for the better.

**You understand why.** As a child, you practised scales because your teacher told you to. As an adult, you understand that scales build technique, train your ear, and prepare you for more complex music. This understanding makes practice more purposeful and less tedious.

**Lessons are collaborative.** A good adult lesson is a conversation, not a lecture. You and your teacher discuss goals, choose repertoire together, and adjust the approach based on what's working. It's a partnership.

**You appreciate music more.** Decades of listening to music give you an intuitive understanding of phrasing, dynamics, and expression that children simply don't have. You might find that you make music sound more musical more quickly than you did the first time around.

**Practice is on your terms.** No one is making you practise. You do it because you want to. This freedom is surprisingly powerful.
