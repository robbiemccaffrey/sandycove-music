Once you've passed your Grade 8, the next step is the diploma — and it's a big one. A diploma is a professional-level music qualification that shows you can perform, teach, or both to a genuinely high standard. It's a serious credential, and earning one is something to be proud of.

The two main diploma options available in Ireland are from ABRSM and Trinity College London. Both are internationally recognised and highly respected, but they work quite differently. Here's what you need to know about each one.

## What Is a Music Diploma?

A diploma sits above the graded exam system. Where Grade 8 demonstrates strong technical and musical ability, a diploma goes further — it requires the kind of musical maturity, interpretive depth, and technical command you'd expect from a professional musician or a highly skilled teacher.

Both ABRSM and Trinity offer diplomas at multiple levels, broadly equivalent to each other:

- **DipABRSM / ATCL** — the first diploma level, roughly equivalent to first-year conservatoire standard
- **LRSM / LTCL** — the licentiate level, equivalent to a graduate-level performer or experienced teacher
- **FRSM / FTCL** — the fellowship level, the highest award either board offers

Most students will start at the first level (DipABRSM or ATCL) and decide from there whether to continue.

## ABRSM Diplomas

ABRSM offers three diploma levels: DipABRSM, LRSM, and FRSM. Each is available in Performance, Teaching, or Music Direction.

### What's involved

For a **performance diploma**, you'll need to prepare a recital programme of a specific duration — around 25–30 minutes for DipABRSM, building up to a full-length recital at FRSM level. Your programme needs to demonstrate variety and musical range, and you'll be assessed on technical command, interpretive understanding, and communication.

There's also a **written component**. At DipABRSM level, this involves programme notes — you'll write about your chosen pieces, discussing their context, structure, and your interpretive choices. At LRSM and above, the written requirements become more substantial.

Additionally, you'll do a **quick study** — a short piece of music you haven't seen before, given to you with limited preparation time. This tests your ability to learn and perform music at sight, which is a different skill from preparing a polished recital.

### What makes ABRSM diplomas distinctive

ABRSM diplomas are thorough. The combination of recital, programme notes, and quick study means you're assessed across multiple skills, not just performance alone. This makes them well-suited to students who are strong all-rounders — good performers who can also think and write about music.

The marking can feel quite exacting. ABRSM examiners are looking for precision, stylistic awareness, and a mature understanding of the repertoire. If you're well prepared, this works in your favour. If there are gaps, they'll be noticed.

## Trinity Diplomas

Trinity offers ATCL, LTCL, and FTCL. Like ABRSM, these are available in Performance and Teaching pathways.

### What's involved

Trinity performance diplomas are focused primarily on the **recital itself**. At ATCL level, you'll perform a programme of around 25–35 minutes. The emphasis is on your ability to communicate musically and build a coherent programme.

One of the key differences is that Trinity diplomas **don't include a separate written paper or quick study** at the lower levels. Your assessment is based on the performance and a brief interview with the examiner afterwards, where you'll discuss your programme and musical choices. This makes the exam itself more streamlined — it's closer to giving a real concert.

Trinity also tends to be more flexible about **repertoire choices**. While you'll still need to demonstrate range and depth, the syllabus is generally more open to contemporary music, cross-genre programming, and less traditional selections. If your strengths lie outside the standard classical canon, Trinity gives you more room to play to them.

### What makes Trinity diplomas distinctive

Trinity's approach is more performance-centred. If you're a strong performer who'd rather let the music speak for itself than write essays about it, Trinity may feel like a better fit. The post-performance interview is conversational — it's a chance to show your understanding of the music without the formality of a written submission.

The marking criteria place a strong emphasis on musicality and communication. Trinity examiners are listening for how well you engage the listener, how thoughtfully you've built your programme, and how convincingly you bring the music to life.

## How Do They Compare?

Here's a practical side-by-side at the first diploma level:

| | **DipABRSM** | **ATCL (Trinity)** |
|---|---|---|
| **Recital length** | ~25–30 minutes | ~25–35 minutes |
| **Written component** | Programme notes (submitted in advance) | No written paper |
| **Quick study** | Yes | No |
| **Post-exam interview** | No | Yes (brief discussion) |
| **Repertoire flexibility** | Classical-focused, structured lists | Broader range of styles accepted |
| **Marking emphasis** | Technical accuracy, stylistic knowledge, written understanding | Musical communication, programme coherence, performance quality |
| **International recognition** | Very strong (90+ countries) | Very strong (widely recognised globally) |

Both qualifications carry equal weight professionally. No employer, university, or conservatoire will consider one inferior to the other. The choice comes down to what suits you as a musician.

## Which One Is Right for You?

There's no universally better option — it genuinely depends on your strengths and preferences.

**ABRSM might suit you better if:**

- You're a well-rounded musician who's strong in aural skills and sight-reading
- You enjoy analysing and writing about music
- You prefer a highly structured exam with clear expectations at every stage
- You want the rigour of being tested across multiple skills, not just performance

**Trinity might suit you better if:**

- Performance is your strongest area and you want it to be the focus
- You prefer talking about music to writing about it
- You'd like more flexibility in your repertoire choices
- You want the exam to feel more like a real concert experience

Some students do both over the course of their studies — perhaps a Trinity ATCL first, then an ABRSM LRSM, or vice versa. The qualifications complement each other well.
