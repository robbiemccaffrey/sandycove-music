If there's one thing that separates students who progress well from those who plateau, it's technique — and technique starts with how you sit and how you hold your hands. Poor posture and hand position are behind most of the tension, pain, and frustration that piano students experience.

The frustrating thing is that bad habits are easy to develop and hard to fix later. Getting it right from the start saves months of remedial work down the line. Whether you're a beginner, a returning player, or someone who's been playing for years with nagging wrist pain, this guide covers everything you need to know.

## Why Posture Matters

Piano might look like a sedentary activity, but it's actually a physical one. Playing involves continuous, precise movement of your fingers, hands, wrists, forearms, and even your upper body. If your body isn't properly aligned, some muscles have to compensate for others, which leads to tension, fatigue, and eventually pain.

Good posture also directly affects your sound. When your body is relaxed and balanced, you can transfer energy from your arms and body weight into the keys efficiently. Tense shoulders or a hunched back restrict your movement and make everything harder than it needs to be.

## Getting the Bench Right

Everything starts with your seating position. Get this wrong and nothing else will feel comfortable.

### Height

Sit so that your forearms are roughly parallel to the floor when your fingers are resting on the keys. Your elbows should be slightly above the level of the keyboard — not below it.

If you're too low, your wrists will be forced upward and your shoulders will hunch. If you're too high, you'll press down on the keys from above, losing control and tiring quickly.

Most piano benches are adjustable. If yours isn't, use a firm cushion to raise yourself up. For children, an adjustable bench is essential — they need to be repositioned as they grow.

### Distance

Sit far enough from the piano that your arms have room to move freely. When your hands are on the keys, your elbows should be slightly in front of your body, not pressed against your sides.

A good test: can you comfortably reach the highest and lowest notes you're playing without leaning or stretching? If you're reaching, move the bench slightly closer. If your elbows are cramped against your body, move back.

### Where to Sit on the Bench

Sit on the front half of the bench, not in the middle or back. This allows you to use your legs and core for balance, and gives you the freedom to lean forward slightly when needed for louder passages or reaches.

Your feet should rest flat on the floor (or on a footrest for younger children), roughly below your knees. This gives you a stable base and access to the pedals.

## Hand Shape and Finger Position

Now for the hands themselves. This is where most problems develop, and where good teaching makes the biggest difference.

### The Natural Hand Shape

The ideal hand position at the piano mirrors the natural shape of your hand when it's completely relaxed. Let your arm hang by your side and look at your hand — your fingers are gently curved, not straight and not clenched. That relaxed curve is what you're aiming for on the keyboard.

Place your hand on the keys as if you're holding a small ball. Your fingers should be curved, your knuckles should be the highest point, and your fingertips should contact the keys — not the pads of your fingers, and certainly not flat fingers.

### Fingertips, Not Flat Fingers

Playing with flat fingers is one of the most common bad habits, especially in self-taught players. Flat fingers give you less control, less power, and more strain on the joints.

When you play a note, the contact point should be the tip of your finger, just where the fingertip meets the nail. Your finger curves down to meet the key. Think of it as pressing, not slapping.

The thumb is the exception — it plays with its side edge rather than its tip, because of how it's angled.

### Collapsed Knuckles

Watch your first (outermost) finger joints. If they collapse inward when you press a key, that's a problem. Collapsed knuckles mean you're pressing with the flat pad of your finger rather than the curved tip. This reduces your control and can cause joint pain over time.

Strengthening this takes awareness and practice. Your teacher can give you specific exercises, but the main fix is simply paying attention to your hand shape during practice and correcting it every time you notice it collapsing.

## Wrist Position and Tension

The wrist is where most piano-related pain and injury occurs. Get this right and you'll avoid the problems that plague many players.

### Neutral Wrist Position

Your wrist should be roughly level with your forearm and the back of your hand — not angled sharply up or down. Imagine a straight line running from your elbow, through your wrist, to your knuckles. That's roughly where you want to be.

A wrist that's too high forces your fingers to reach down to the keys, reducing control. A wrist that's too low (dropping below the keyboard level) puts strain on the tendons and makes fast passages difficult.

### Wrist Flexibility

While your wrist position should be neutral at rest, it shouldn't be rigid. The wrist needs to move — small rotational movements, gentle up-and-down motions for phrasing, lateral shifts to move across the keyboard. A locked wrist forces your fingers to do all the work, which leads to fatigue and tension.

Think of the wrist as a shock absorber. It should be firm enough to support your hand but flexible enough to absorb the impact of playing and facilitate smooth movement.

### Signs of Wrist Tension

If you experience any of the following, wrist tension is likely the cause:

- Aching or pain in the wrist during or after playing
- Stiffness in the fingers after practice
- A feeling of "tightness" in the forearm
- Difficulty playing fast passages smoothly
- Fatigue that sets in quickly

The fix is almost always the same: slow down, check your posture, relax your shoulders, and ensure your wrist is in a neutral position. If pain persists, stop playing and consult a healthcare professional before continuing.

## Common Posture Mistakes

### Hunched Shoulders

Tension in the shoulders is incredibly common, especially when playing difficult passages or when nervous. Hunched or raised shoulders restrict arm movement, create tension that travels down to the hands, and cause neck and back pain.

Check in with your shoulders regularly during practice. Drop them. Consciously relax them. If you notice them creeping up, pause, take a breath, shake out your arms, and reset.

### Leaning Too Far Forward

Some students lean over the keyboard as if trying to get closer to the music. This puts strain on your lower back and restricts your arm movement. Sit upright with a slight, natural lean forward from the hips — not from the shoulders.

### Locked Elbows

Your elbows should be free to move. If they're locked against your sides, your arms can't shift laterally to reach different parts of the keyboard. Keep a small gap between your elbows and your body, and let your arms move naturally.

### Gripping the Keys

This is subtle but common. Some students press the keys with more force than necessary, essentially "gripping" the keyboard. Piano keys require very little force to depress. If your fingers are white-knuckled or your forearms feel tense, you're working too hard.

Practice playing as quietly as possible. This forces you to use minimal force and develops a light, controlled touch.

## Stretches and Warm-Ups for Pianists

Just as you'd warm up before exercise, a brief warm-up before playing helps prevent strain and gets your hands ready.

### Before Playing

- **Finger stretches:** Spread your fingers wide, hold for 5 seconds, then make a fist. Repeat 5 times.
- **Wrist circles:** Extend your arm and rotate your wrist slowly in circles, 10 times in each direction.
- **Forearm stretch:** Extend one arm straight out, palm up. With the other hand, gently pull your fingers back towards you. Hold for 15 seconds. Repeat palm down.
- **Shoulder rolls:** Roll your shoulders forward 5 times, then backward 5 times.

### During Practice

If you're practising for more than 20-30 minutes, take short breaks. Stand up, stretch your hands, roll your shoulders, and walk around for a minute. This prevents the gradual build-up of tension that leads to discomfort and injury.

## When to Seek Help

If you experience persistent pain while playing piano — in your wrists, hands, forearms, shoulders, or back — don't ignore it. Pain is your body telling you something is wrong.

Common causes include poor technique, an improperly adjusted bench, practising too long without breaks, or suddenly increasing your practice time dramatically. A good teacher can usually identify and correct the issue. If pain persists despite technique adjustments, see a physiotherapist or doctor who has experience with musicians.
