Not everyone can make it to a studio every week. Whether you're busy with work, live outside South Dublin, or simply prefer learning from home — online piano lessons are a genuine option that works well for many students.

At Sandycove School of Music, we've been offering lessons over Zoom alongside our in-person sessions for some time now. Here's an honest look at how online lessons work and whether they might suit you.

## How Online Piano Lessons Work

The setup is simple. You sit at your piano or keyboard at home, connect via Zoom (or similar), and your teacher guides you through the lesson in real time — just as they would in person. You can see your teacher demonstrating, and they can watch your hands, posture, and technique through your camera.

A typical online lesson follows the same structure as an in-person one:

- Warm-up and technique exercises
- Working through current pieces
- Sight-reading or theory as needed
- Setting goals for the week ahead

The main difference is the medium, not the quality. A good teacher adapts their approach to work well on screen.

## What You Need

You don't need an expensive setup. The essentials are:

- **A piano or keyboard** — An acoustic piano, digital piano, or a decent keyboard with weighted or semi-weighted keys. If you're buying your first instrument, a digital piano with 88 weighted keys is the best starting point.
- **A device with a camera** — A laptop, tablet, or phone. Laptops tend to work best because they're stable and give a good camera angle.
- **Decent internet** — A standard home broadband connection is fine. You don't need anything special.
- **Camera positioning** — Ideally, your device should be positioned so the teacher can see your hands on the keys. A side angle (from the music stand area) usually works well. Some students use a phone on a small tripod alongside a laptop.

That's it. No special software, no expensive microphones. Zoom's free plan is more than adequate for a lesson.

## Who Are Online Lessons Best For?

Online lessons work well for a wide range of students. They're particularly suited to:

- **Adults with busy schedules** — No commute time means it's easier to fit a lesson into a working day. Some of our online students take lessons during their lunch break.
- **Students outside South Dublin** — We teach students from across Ireland and occasionally abroad. Geography isn't a barrier.
- **Teenagers** — Especially those preparing for Leaving Cert or Junior Cycle Music who are already juggling school, study, and other commitments.
- **Returning players** — If you played years ago and want to pick it back up, the convenience of online lessons can lower the barrier to getting started.
- **Parents of young children** — If getting out of the house for a lesson is difficult, having the teacher come to you (virtually) makes a real difference.

## Are Online Lessons as Good as In-Person?

This is the question everyone asks, and the honest answer is: for most students, yes — with some nuances.

**What works just as well online:**
- Learning new pieces and working through repertoire
- Music theory and sight-reading
- Exam preparation (technique, musicality, programme planning)
- Discussing goals, reviewing progress, and planning practice

**Where in-person has a slight edge:**
- Very young beginners (ages 5–6) can benefit from the physical presence of a teacher helping with hand position and posture
- Advanced students working on subtle tonal and dynamic nuances may prefer being in the same room as the teacher and a good instrument

For the vast majority of students — beginners through to advanced, children through to adults — online lessons deliver the same results. The key factor is the quality of the teaching, not the medium.

## Online Exam Preparation

If you're preparing for grade exams (RIAM, ABRSM, or Trinity) or state exams (Junior Cycle, Leaving Cert), online lessons cover everything you need. We regularly prepare students for exams entirely over Zoom — piece selection, technique, sight-reading, aural skills, and exam strategy.

The exams themselves are still taken in person at an exam centre, but all of the preparation can be done online without any disadvantage.

## Tips for Getting the Most from Online Lessons

A few practical things that make online lessons work better:

- **Practise between lessons** — This is true for in-person too, but it matters even more online. The lesson is where your teacher identifies what to work on; the real progress happens when you practise.
- **Minimise distractions** — Close the door, put your phone on silent, and let your household know you're in a lesson. Thirty minutes of focused attention is worth more than an hour of distracted playing.
- **Good lighting** — Make sure your teacher can see your hands clearly. Natural light or a desk lamp angled towards the keyboard helps.
- **Arrive ready** — Have your music out, your piano uncovered, and Zoom open before the lesson starts. It sounds small, but it makes a difference when you're working within a set time.
