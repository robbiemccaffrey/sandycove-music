It's one of the most common questions we get from parents: "Is my child old enough to start piano lessons?" The short answer is that most children are ready to begin between the ages of 5 and 7, but the slightly longer answer is that readiness matters more than age.

## The Sweet Spot: Ages 5 to 7

For most children, somewhere between 5 and 7 is ideal for starting piano lessons. By this age, children generally have the fine motor skills needed to press individual keys, the attention span for a 30-minute lesson, and enough cognitive development to understand basic musical concepts like rhythm and melody.

That said, every child is different. Some 5-year-olds are absolutely ready and eager to learn, while some 7-year-olds might need a gentler introduction. There's no magic number — it depends on the child.

## Signs Your Child Might Be Ready

Rather than focusing purely on age, look for these signs of readiness:

- **They show interest in music** — singing along to songs, tapping out rhythms, or asking about instruments
- **They can sit and focus** for at least 15-20 minutes on a single activity
- **They can follow simple instructions** and are comfortable in a one-on-one setting with an adult
- **Their hands are big enough** to comfortably rest on the keys — this is a practical consideration that's easy to overlook
- **They want to learn** — this is the most important one. A child who genuinely wants to play piano will always progress faster than one who's been told they have to

## What About Starting Earlier?

Some parents ask about starting at age 3 or 4. While there are programmes designed for very young children, formal one-on-one piano lessons at this age can be counterproductive. Very young children often lack the fine motor control and concentration needed, and pushing too early can turn music into a chore rather than a joy.

If your child is under 5 and showing strong musical interest, a group music class focused on singing, rhythm, and movement is usually a better fit. These classes build a fantastic musical foundation that will make formal piano lessons much easier when the time comes.

## Starting Later Is Perfectly Fine

On the other end, there's absolutely nothing wrong with starting piano at age 8, 10, 12, or any age at all. Older children often progress quickly because they have stronger concentration, better motor skills, and a clearer understanding of what they want to achieve.

We've had students start at 10 and reach Grade 5 within a couple of years because they were motivated and practised consistently. Don't worry if your child hasn't started yet — it's never too late.

## Making Those First Lessons Count

If your child is ready to start, here's how to set them up for success:

**Keep it short.** A 30-minute lesson is plenty for young beginners. It keeps things focused and prevents fatigue.

**Make practice a habit, not a battle.** Even 10-15 minutes of daily practice is far more effective than one long session. Try to make it part of the routine — after school, before dinner, whatever works for your family.

**Celebrate progress.** Learning an instrument is a long game. Celebrate small wins — learning a new piece, mastering a tricky passage, or just showing up consistently.

**Let them enjoy it.** The goal of early lessons isn't to produce a concert pianist — it's to build a love of music that lasts a lifetime. The technique and the grades will come naturally if the enjoyment is there first.
