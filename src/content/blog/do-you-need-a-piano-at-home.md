It's one of the first questions parents ask when considering piano lessons for their child, and one that adult beginners ask themselves: "Do I need to have a piano at home?"

The short answer is: yes, you need something to practise on. But it doesn't have to be a grand piano, and it doesn't have to cost a fortune. Let's break down what you actually need, what your options are, and how to make a smart choice at any budget.

## Can You Learn Piano Without One at Home?

Technically, you can attend lessons without an instrument at home. But realistically, your progress will be extremely limited.

Piano lessons give you the tools and direction. Practice is where the actual learning happens. A 30-minute weekly lesson needs to be supported by regular daily practice for the concepts and techniques to stick. Without an instrument at home, you'll spend most of each lesson re-learning what was covered the week before, rather than building on it.

We've seen this many times at Sandycove School of Music. Students who practise regularly at home — even for just 15-20 minutes a day — make dramatically faster progress than those who only play during their lesson. It's not even close.

So while having a piano at home isn't technically mandatory, it's practically essential if you want to make meaningful progress.

## Digital vs Acoustic: Which Should You Choose?

This is the big decision, and the right answer depends on your budget, your space, and how committed you (or your child) are to learning.

### Acoustic Pianos

An acoustic piano — whether upright or grand — produces sound through hammers striking strings. The feel, tone, and responsiveness are what digital pianos try to replicate.

**Pros:**
- Superior touch and tone quality
- No electronics to break or become obsolete
- Holds value well (quality uprights can last decades)
- More inspiring to play — there's something about the resonance of a real piano

**Cons:**
- Expensive to buy (€1,500-€5,000+ for a decent upright, much more for a grand)
- Heavy and difficult to move
- Requires regular tuning (€80-€120 once or twice a year)
- Can't be played silently (no headphone option on most models)
- Needs a stable environment — humidity and temperature changes affect tuning

**Best for:** Students who are committed to long-term playing, have the space and budget, and want the authentic piano experience.

### Digital Pianos

A digital piano uses electronic samples and weighted keys to simulate the feel and sound of an acoustic piano. The best ones are very convincing.

**Pros:**
- Much more affordable (€350-€800 for a good one)
- Headphone jack for silent practice (a lifesaver in apartments and late-night practice)
- Never needs tuning
- Lighter and more portable
- Often includes additional sounds, a metronome, and recording functions

**Cons:**
- The touch isn't quite the same as an acoustic piano (though high-end models come very close)
- Sound is produced through speakers, which don't have the natural resonance of strings
- Electronics have a limited lifespan compared to a well-maintained acoustic instrument
- Can feel less "special" or inspiring to some players

**Best for:** Beginners, families testing whether a child will stick with lessons, adults with space or noise constraints, and anyone on a budget.

### What About Keyboards?

A keyboard (the type you might find for €100-€200 at an electronics shop) is not the same as a digital piano. Most cheap keyboards have:

- Unweighted keys that feel nothing like a real piano
- Fewer than 88 keys
- A light, springy touch that doesn't develop proper finger technique

If you're serious about learning piano, avoid unweighted keyboards. They'll actually hinder your progress because the technique you develop on them doesn't transfer well to a real piano. Your teacher will be working on touch, dynamics, and control — none of which you can practise on a toy keyboard.

That said, a keyboard is better than nothing for the first week or two while you're deciding whether to commit. Just don't let it become a permanent substitute.

## What to Look for in a Digital Piano

If you're going the digital route (which is what we recommend for most beginners), here are the features that matter:

### Weighted Keys (Non-Negotiable)

This is the single most important feature. Weighted (or "hammer action") keys simulate the resistance of an acoustic piano. When you press a key, it should feel substantial — heavier in the bass, lighter in the treble, with a realistic "escapement" feel.

Semi-weighted keys are a compromise and better than nothing, but fully weighted hammer-action keys are what you should aim for.

### 88 Keys

A full-size piano has 88 keys. Some portable keyboards have 61 or 76. For serious piano study, you need all 88. You might not use the extreme ends often as a beginner, but you will as you progress, and you don't want to have to upgrade within a year.

### Touch Sensitivity

The keys should respond to how hard you press them — play gently and the sound is soft, play firmly and it's loud. This is essential for developing dynamics and musical expression. Any decent digital piano will have this, but check that it feels natural and responsive.

### A Good Piano Sound

Listen to the built-in piano sound. It should be clear, realistic, and pleasant to listen to. Many digital pianos also include other instrument sounds, but the quality of the main piano tone is what matters most.

### A Sustain Pedal

Most digital pianos come with a basic sustain pedal. This is essential — the sustain pedal is used constantly in piano playing, even at beginner level.

## Budget Recommendations

Here's what you can expect at different price points in 2026:

### Under €400

At this price, you're looking at entry-level digital pianos with weighted keys. Brands like Yamaha (P-45 or its successor), Casio (CDP-S110), and Roland offer models in this range. They won't have the most realistic touch or the richest sound, but they're perfectly adequate for beginners and will serve you well for the first few years.

### €400-€700

This is the sweet spot for most piano students. Models like the Yamaha P-225, Casio PX-S1100, or Roland FP-30X offer excellent weighted keys, good sound quality, Bluetooth connectivity, and useful practice features. A piano in this range will last you through the early grades comfortably and still be enjoyable for intermediate playing.

### €700-€1,200

At this level, you're getting into more advanced digital pianos with superior key action, better speaker systems, and richer sound sampling. Models like the Kawai ES520, Yamaha P-525, or Roland FP-60X are popular choices. These are suitable for advancing students and adults who want a more authentic experience without buying an acoustic instrument.

### Over €1,200

If you're investing more, you might consider a digital piano with a cabinet stand (looks like a small upright), or a good quality second-hand acoustic upright. At this budget, an acoustic piano becomes a realistic option and worth considering if you have the space.

## Second-Hand Acoustic Pianos

A decent second-hand upright piano can be surprisingly affordable — sometimes €500-€1,500 for a well-maintained instrument. If you have the space and don't mind occasional tuning costs, a second-hand acoustic can be excellent value.

**Things to check when buying second-hand:**
- Have it inspected by a piano technician (€50-€80 well spent)
- Check for sticking keys, dead notes, and uneven tone
- Look at the hammers — are they grooved and worn, or still in decent shape?
- Consider moving costs — pianos are heavy and specialist movers charge €150-€300

## When to Upgrade

You don't need to buy the perfect piano from day one. Start with what fits your budget, and upgrade when:

- Your child has been playing consistently for a year or more and is clearly committed
- You or your child is preparing for Grade 5+ exams and the instrument's limitations are becoming noticeable
- You've outgrown the touch and sound of your current instrument and it's affecting your motivation

Many families start with a €400-€500 digital piano and upgrade to a better digital or an acoustic instrument after two or three years, once they know the commitment is real. That's a perfectly sensible approach.
