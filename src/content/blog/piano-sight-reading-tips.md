Sight-reading is the skill most piano students dread, and the one most teachers wish their students would practise more. It's also one of the most genuinely useful things you can learn as a pianist — not just for exams, but for everyday musical life.

Being able to sit down and play a piece of music you've never seen before, even roughly, opens up a world of possibilities. You can learn new repertoire faster, join ensembles without weeks of preparation, accompany singers or instrumentalists at short notice, and explore music freely without the commitment of "properly" learning every piece.

Here's how to get better at it.

## Why Sight-Reading Feels So Hard

Sight-reading asks you to do several things simultaneously: read the notes, interpret the rhythm, maintain a steady tempo, use correct fingering, and coordinate both hands — all in real time, with no opportunity to stop and figure things out.

When you're learning a piece normally, you can slow down, repeat difficult bars, practise hands separately, and build up gradually. Sight-reading doesn't allow any of that. You read it and you play it, mistakes and all.

This is why it feels so much harder than playing pieces you know. It genuinely is harder in the moment — but the skill improves dramatically with regular practice, and the progress is faster than most students expect.

## The Foundation: Read Ahead

The single most important sight-reading skill is reading ahead. Your eyes should always be at least one beat ahead of your fingers — ideally a whole bar ahead.

This sounds impossible at first, but it's the same skill you use when reading aloud from a book. Your eyes are already on the next word while your mouth is saying the current one. Music reading works the same way once you've practised enough.

How to develop this:

- **Start with very simple pieces** — well below your playing level. If you're working on Grade 4 repertoire, sight-read at Grade 1 level. The simpler the music, the easier it is to look ahead.
- **Cover the bar you've just played** with a piece of card, so you can't look back. This forces your eyes forward.
- **Don't stop for mistakes.** The most important rule of sight-reading is to keep going. If you hit a wrong note, let it go and stay with the rhythm. Stopping to correct breaks the flow and teaches your brain that it's acceptable to pause.

## Step-by-Step Approach to a New Piece

When you sit down with a piece you've never seen before — whether in practice or an exam — take 30 seconds to scan it before you play a note. Here's what to look for:

### 1. Key Signature and Time Signature

Check the sharps or flats at the start. If there are two sharps, you're in D major (or B minor). Make a mental note of which notes are affected — you don't want to be surprised by an F sharp in bar 8 that you forgot about.

Check the time signature. Is it 3/4, 4/4, 6/8? This tells you the rhythmic feel and where the strong beats fall.

### 2. Tempo and Character

Look at any tempo markings or expressive directions. Allegro? Andante? This sets your pace. In sight-reading, it's always better to choose a slightly slower tempo than marked and play steadily than to attempt full speed and fall apart.

### 3. Patterns and Repetition

Scan the piece for repeated sections, sequences, and patterns. Most sight-reading pieces at grade level contain a lot of repetition. If you notice that bars 5-8 are the same as bars 1-4 but in a different hand, that's half the piece already understood.

### 4. Tricky Spots

Identify any rhythmic complications, accidentals, hand position changes, or jumps. Knowing where the difficult bits are before you start means they won't catch you off guard.

### 5. Fingering

Quickly plan your starting hand position for each hand. If the right hand starts on middle C with the thumb, where will it need to be by bar 4? Planning even rough fingering saves you from awkward position changes mid-piece.

## Daily Sight-Reading Practice

Sight-reading improves with consistent practice — even just five minutes a day makes a noticeable difference within weeks. Here's how to structure it.

### Find Material

You need a constant supply of music you haven't seen before. Options include:

- **Sight-reading books** designed for your grade level (many are available for RIAM, ABRSM, and Trinity syllabuses)
- **Grade books from lower levels** — if you're working on Grade 5, sight-read from Grade 2 or 3 books
- **Hymn books or folk song collections** — simple, predictable structures that are perfect for practice
- **Sheet music apps** that generate new exercises

The key is that the music should be below your playing level. Sight-reading practice works when you can play most of it correctly. If you're struggling with every bar, the music is too difficult for this purpose.

### A Five-Minute Daily Routine

1. Pick a piece you've never played (30 seconds below your current grade level is ideal)
2. Scan it for 30 seconds using the step-by-step approach above
3. Set a steady tempo — slower than you think
4. Play it through without stopping, mistakes and all
5. Play it once more, slightly faster, trying to correct what went wrong

That's it. Five minutes. Do this every day and you'll be a noticeably better sight-reader within a month.

## Common Sight-Reading Mistakes

### Stopping to Fix Errors

This is the number one mistake. When you stop to correct a wrong note, you lose the rhythmic flow and practise the habit of stopping. In an exam, stopping costs you more marks than a wrong note does. Train yourself to keep going no matter what.

### Looking at Your Hands

You can't read ahead if you're looking at the keyboard. For simple passages, trust your hands to find the right keys without visual confirmation. This takes practice, but it's essential for fluent sight-reading.

Practise playing simple scales and exercises without looking at your hands. Your sense of the keyboard geography develops quickly.

### Trying to Play Every Note Perfectly

Sight-reading is not about perfection. It's about conveying the general shape, rhythm, and character of the music. If a chord has four notes and you can only grab three, that's fine. If the left hand part is too complex, simplify it to the bass note on each beat. Getting the rhythm and overall feel right matters more than hitting every single note.

### Ignoring the Left Hand

Many students sight-read the right hand melody and forget or simplify the left hand too much. In exams, both hands are assessed. Make sure you practise sight-reading with both hands from the start, even if the left hand is basic.

### Not Practising Regularly

Sight-reading is a skill that improves with frequency, not intensity. One five-minute session every day is far more effective than a 35-minute session once a week. Build it into your daily [practice routine](/blog/how-to-practice-piano-effectively).

## Sight-Reading in Exams

Sight-reading is a component of every major piano exam — RIAM, ABRSM, and Trinity all include it. Here's what you need to know for each.

### RIAM

Sight-reading is part of the "Musicianship" section. You're given a short piece to play after a brief preparation time. The difficulty increases with each grade. RIAM sight-reading tests tend to focus on melodic and rhythmic accuracy.

### ABRSM

ABRSM allocates 21 marks out of 150 for sight-reading. You get 30 seconds of preparation time. The test is a short piece appropriate to your grade level, and you're assessed on pitch, rhythm, continuity, and some awareness of dynamics and phrasing.

### Trinity

Trinity includes sight-reading as part of the exam, with the difficulty calibrated to the grade. Trinity's sight-reading tests often include slightly more musical detail (dynamics, articulation) than some other boards, so it's worth practising with expression as well as accuracy.

For all three boards, the best preparation is the daily practice routine described above. Students who sight-read regularly perform dramatically better in this component than those who only attempt it in the final weeks before the exam.

## The Bigger Picture

Sight-reading isn't just an exam requirement — it's a life skill for musicians. The student who can sight-read confidently can sit down at any piano, open any book of music, and play something enjoyable. That's a level of musical freedom that's genuinely rewarding.

Like any skill, it starts out feeling difficult and gradually becomes natural. The investment is tiny — five minutes a day — and the payoff is enormous.
